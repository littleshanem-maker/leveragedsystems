How to Claim Variations in Construction: A Subcontractor's Complete Guide

Shane Little February 2026 10 min read

Variations are an inevitable part of construction. Whether it's a design change, unexpected site conditions, or additional work requested by the client, knowing how to claim variations properly can mean the difference between a profitable project and a costly dispute.

For Australian subcontractors operating on tight margins, getting the variation process right isn't optional — it's essential. This guide walks through the complete process, from identifying a variation on-site to getting it paid.

What Actually Constitutes a Construction Variation?

A variation occurs when any of the following happen:

  • The scope of work changes from the original contract
  • Additional work is requested beyond what was contracted
  • Work is deleted or reduced from the original scope
  • The method of construction is altered
  • Site conditions differ significantly from those anticipated
  • Access to the work area is restricted or delayed
  • The sequence of work must change due to circumstances beyond your control

Common real-world examples: excavation revealing unexpected rock or contaminated soil, design changes requiring different materials, additional electrical points requested after walls are closed, discovery of existing services not shown on plans.

Key principle: You don't need to wait for formal instruction to identify a variation. As soon as site conditions change from what the contract assumed, start your documentation process — even before formal approval.

Step 1: Document Everything From Day One

1 Foundation

Successful variation claims start before any variation actually occurs. Establish documentation practices from project commencement — not after something goes wrong.

Pre-construction:

  • Photograph the entire site before work begins — ground conditions, existing structures, access paths
  • Keep copies of every drawing, specification, and instruction received
  • Note any discrepancies between contract documents — in writing, to the principal

Daily, throughout the project:

  • Maintain site diaries: weather, workforce numbers, work completed, any verbal instructions received
  • Photograph progress and any issues immediately — timestamps matter
  • Record all verbal instructions on the day they're given

This baseline becomes your evidence when you need to demonstrate that conditions or scope changed from the original contract.

Step 2: Identify the Variation Early

2 Identification

Train your site team to recognise variation triggers. The earlier you identify one, the better your position — both for securing approval and for protecting your programme.

Warning signs to watch for:

  • Verbal instructions to do work differently than specified
  • Requests for additional work not in the original scope
  • Site conditions that don't match contract documents
  • Design changes communicated informally (WhatsApp, verbal on-site)
  • Access restrictions or delays caused by other trades

When you identify a variation trigger, stop and assess. Don't just absorb it into the work — flag it immediately.

Step 3: Provide Formal, Prompt Written Notice

3 Notification

This is the step most subcontractors get wrong, and it's the one that kills legitimate claims. Most contracts include strict timeframes for variation notification — 24 hours, 48 hours, or 5 business days are common. Miss the window and you may lose your right to claim, regardless of merit.

A proper variation notice includes:

  • Reference to the specific contract clause requiring notification
  • Clear description of the circumstances giving rise to the variation
  • Whether it's a principal-directed variation or a circumstance beyond your control
  • Initial estimate of cost and time impact (you can refine this later)
  • A request for written instruction to proceed

Here's a simple format that works:

RE: VARIATION NOTIFICATION — [Project Name] Contract: [Contract Number] Date: [Date] We provide notice under clause [X] of a variation arising from [circumstances]. Description: [What changed / what additional work is required] Cause: [Why this constitutes a variation] Estimated Additional Cost: $[Amount] (subject to detailed assessment) Estimated Time Impact: [Days/weeks, if applicable] Supporting Docs: [List any photos, drawings, or evidence attached] We request written instruction to proceed with this varied work.

Email is ideal — it creates an automatic timestamp and delivery record. If verbal instructions are given in urgent situations, send a confirming email within 24 hours.

Step 4: Get Written Approval Before Proceeding

4 Approval

Never proceed with significant variation work based solely on verbal approval. It protects both parties — and in practice, clients who aren't willing to put approval in writing often aren't planning to pay.

Written approval should include:

  • Clear description of the varied work authorised
  • The agreed method of pricing (daywork, schedule rates, or lump sum quotation)
  • Acknowledgment of any time extensions
  • Any conditions or limitations on the approval

Urgent situations: If you must proceed before written approval is available, confirm the verbal instruction in writing within 24 hours: "This is to confirm your instruction to [proceed with X]. We will proceed on this basis. Please confirm your approval in writing at your earliest convenience."

Step 5: Price the Variation Accurately

5 Pricing

How you price variation work significantly affects both profitability and the likelihood of approval. Most contracts specify a hierarchy for variation assessment:

  1. Schedule Rates — rates from your original tender (be careful here; see below)
  2. Daywork — actual costs plus agreed margins
  3. Quotation — for complex variations requiring detailed assessment
  4. Fair and reasonable rates — market rates where other methods don't apply

Cost components to include in every variation:

  • Direct labour including all on-costs (super, workers' comp, payroll tax)
  • Materials at current market rates — not rates from months ago
  • Plant and equipment costs
  • Site overheads and preliminaries
  • Head office overhead recovery
  • Profit margin
  • GST where applicable

The schedule rates trap: Original tender rates assumed efficient, planned production. Variation work often costs more — it interrupts sequences, requires short-notice procurement, and creates inefficiency. You may be entitled to claim disruption and inefficiency costs on top of schedule rates.

Step 6: Track Every Dollar of Variation Work

6 Cost Tracking

Once approved, treat variation work with the same rigour as your original contract work — with separate cost codes:

  • Daily progress photos showing variation work specifically
  • Timesheets with variation-specific cost codes for every worker
  • Material delivery records and quantities consumed
  • Plant hire records allocated to the variation
  • Any delays or disruptions that affect variation completion

Reconstruct costs after the fact — even a few weeks later — and accuracy drops significantly. Capture in real time.

Step 7: Submit a Professional Claim

7 Claim Submission

A well-presented variation claim gets paid faster and with less pushback. Structure your submission as:

  • Executive summary: one page, variation ID, date, amount, status
  • Reference to approval: copy of the written instruction
  • Cost breakdown: labour, materials, plant — itemised and supported
  • Progress photos: showing work in progress and completed
  • Time extension request: if applicable, with programme impact analysis
  • Total amount including GST

Submit within required timeframes, keep a copy of everything you send, and follow up to confirm receipt.

When Your Claim Is Disputed

Not every variation gets paid without pushback. When disputes arise, follow a structured escalation:

  1. Good faith negotiation — present your evidence professionally at a meeting. Be commercial, not emotional.
  2. Senior management — escalate if project-level resolution fails. Bring your documentation organised and clear.
  3. Adjudication — Security of Payment legislation in every Australian state gives you the right to rapid adjudication. It's faster and cheaper than litigation, and doesn't require lawyers for straightforward claims.
  4. Court or arbitration — last resort, significant cost, but sometimes necessary for large amounts.

Adjudication is underused: Many subcontractors don't know this option exists. An adjudicator can issue a binding determination — typically within 10 business days — requiring the principal to pay. If your documentation is solid, this is often the fastest path to payment.

Managing Relationships Through the Process

Professional variation management strengthens relationships — it doesn't damage them. Clients who've been through disputes with disorganised subcontractors actually prefer working with contractors who handle variations systematically, because it removes uncertainty on both sides.

Stay cooperative and solution-focused throughout. Separate the commercial discussion from the working relationship. Be honest about costs and time impacts. Most variation disputes don't need to become adversarial — they arise from misunderstanding and poor communication, not bad faith.
