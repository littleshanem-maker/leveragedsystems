Variation Agreement Template Australia: What to Include and Why

Shane Little February 2026 11 min read

In Australian construction, a well-crafted variation agreement template can mean the difference between smooth project delivery and a costly dispute. For subcontractors operating with tight margins, having the right variation agreement framework isn't just good practice — it's essential business protection.

This guide breaks down what makes an effective construction variation template, why each element matters, and how Australian subcontractors can use proper documentation to protect their interests while maintaining productive client relationships.

What Is a Variation Agreement?

A variation agreement is a formal document that records changes to the original construction contract. Unlike a simple variation order, it establishes the complete framework for how variations will be identified, approved, priced, and executed — before disputes arise.

In Australia's construction industry, variation agreements must operate within:

  • Australian Consumer Law requirements
  • State-based building and construction legislation
  • Security of Payment Acts (which differ by state)
  • Fair Trading legislation

The complexity of this regulatory environment makes well-drafted templates invaluable for subcontractors who can't afford bespoke legal advice on every project.

Core Element #1: Definitions

Element 01

Your template should begin with clear definitions that eliminate ambiguity before it starts. Ambiguous language is the primary source of variation disputes.

Essential terms to define:

  • "Variation" — any addition to, omission from, or change to the Contract Works, including changes to sequence, method, or timing
  • "Principal" — the party engaging your services (and who can authorise variations)
  • "Contract Works" — the original scope of services you were engaged to perform
  • "Daywork" — time-based charging method (hours × agreed rates)
  • "Schedule Rates" — the pre-agreed rates from your original quotation

Why this matters: "Variation" is commonly interpreted differently by principals and subcontractors. A clear, broad definition in your template protects you from arguments that certain scope changes "aren't really variations." If work wasn't in the original scope, it's a variation — your template should say so explicitly.

Core Element #2: Authority and Authorisation

Element 02

Your template must clearly establish who can authorise variations and what form that authorisation must take. Verbal authorisation from a site supervisor is not the same as written authorisation from the project manager — but without a clear clause, disputes arise about which one counts.

Key provisions:

  • Named roles (not individuals) who can authorise variations — project manager, superintendent, or their designated representative
  • Required format for variation instructions — written, signed, including a description and estimated cost acknowledgment
  • Process for urgent variations where written approval can't be obtained immediately
  • Consequences when work proceeds under instruction from an unauthorised person
Sample clause: Only the Project Manager or their nominated representative may authorise variations. All variation instructions must be provided in writing and include: — Description of the varied work — Reason for the variation — Whether time extension is acknowledged — Any conditions or limitations on the approval

Core Element #3: Notice Requirements

Element 03

Australian construction contracts typically include strict notification requirements. Your variation agreement template should specify exactly what's required — and what happens if notice isn't provided.

Notice provisions to include:

  • Timeframe for providing notice — typically 5 business days from becoming aware of the variation circumstance
  • Required content of the notice — description, estimated cost, estimated time impact, supporting evidence
  • Approved methods of delivery — email is recommended (automatic timestamp)
  • What happens if the principal doesn't respond — deemed approval after X days
  • Emergency procedures for urgent variations that require immediate action

Note: Your template's notice requirements should be longer than any contract you sign with a principal. If the principal's contract requires 48 hours notice, your template framework should still default to "give notice immediately" — so that whatever the contract says, your process ensures you'll meet it.

Core Element #4: Pricing Methodology

Element 04

This is often the most contested element of variation agreements. A clear pricing hierarchy — agreed upfront — removes most of the argument.

Standard pricing hierarchy (in order of precedence):

  1. Schedule Rates — pre-agreed rates from your original tender where applicable
  2. Daywork — actual costs at agreed rates, used where schedule rates don't apply
  3. Quotation — for complex variations requiring detailed assessment before approval
  4. Fair and reasonable rates — market rates where none of the above apply

Cost components that must be explicitly included:

  • Direct labour including superannuation, workers' compensation, and payroll tax
  • Materials at current market rates (not historic rates from the original tender)
  • Plant and equipment hire
  • Site overheads and preliminaries proportional to the variation
  • Head office overhead recovery (typically a percentage of direct costs)
  • Profit margin
  • GST

Specify each of these explicitly in your template. If you don't, a principal can argue that your rates include everything — when they clearly don't.

Core Element #5: Time Extensions

Element 05

Variations often affect project timing. Without a clear time extension clause, you can end up with liquidated damages applied to work that was actually delayed by the client's own variation instructions.

Time-related provisions to include:

  • Automatic entitlement to time extensions for principal-directed variations
  • Assessment methodology — how time impact is calculated (critical path, sequential impact)
  • Seasonal and weather considerations
  • Additional costs for acceleration if the principal directs you to maintain the programme despite scope additions
Sample clause: Where a variation increases the quantity of work or introduces new activities, the Subcontractor is entitled to a fair and reasonable extension of time. Time extensions will be assessed considering: a) Direct time impact of the varied work b) Impact on critical path activities c) Resource availability and mobilisation d) Interface with other trades or contractor works

Core Element #6: Documentation Requirements

Element 06

Specify what records must be kept for variation work. This protects you in two ways: it ensures your team captures what's needed for your claim, and it sets expectations with the principal about what you'll be providing.

Documentation requirements to specify:

  • Daily workforce records with variation-specific cost codes
  • Material delivery records and consumption tracking
  • Plant and equipment utilisation records
  • Progress photographs at specified intervals
  • Records of any delays, disruptions, or quality issues affecting variation work
  • Timeframe for making records available — typically within 5 business days of request

Core Element #7: Payment Terms

Element 07

Variation work creates cash flow risk if payment terms aren't specified. Your template should include:

  • When variation work is included in progress payment claims — typically the next progress claim after completion or monthly, whichever is first
  • Retention arrangements — if variation work is subject to retention or not
  • Interest on late payments — aligns with your contract and Security of Payment Act provisions
  • Right to suspend work for non-payment of approved variations
  • Adjudication rights under Security of Payment legislation

Core Element #8: Dispute Resolution

Element 08

Even with comprehensive agreements, disputes arise. Your template should include a structured escalation path:

  1. Good faith negotiation — direct discussion between project managers within 10 business days
  2. Senior management escalation — if project-level resolution fails within 15 days
  3. Adjudication — under relevant state Security of Payment legislation
  4. Expert determination — for technical disputes where an independent expert's view is needed
  5. Arbitration or litigation — as a last resort

Common Pitfalls in Variation Templates

Pitfall 1: Undefined "variation"

Templates that don't clearly define what constitutes a variation leave too much room for argument. Define it broadly — and include examples.

Pitfall 2: Unrealistic notice timeframes

Templates that impose very short notice windows (24 hours for complex circumstances) often can't be met in practice, weakening your own position when you miss them.

Pitfall 3: Schedule rates without disruption carve-outs

Pricing variations purely at schedule rates ignores the real cost of disruption and inefficiency. Include an explicit right to claim additional disruption costs.

Pitfall 4: No deemed approval mechanism

If the principal doesn't respond to a variation notice, work can stall indefinitely. Include a deemed approval provision — e.g., "if no response within 5 business days, the variation is deemed approved on the terms notified."

Pitfall 5: Inconsistent with Security of Payment rights

Your template cannot override statutory rights under Security of Payment legislation. Make sure your template aligns with — and references — your statutory rights rather than inadvertently waiving them.

Implementing Your Variation Template

Having a template is only half the job. Effective implementation requires:

  • Team training — every site supervisor needs to understand what a variation trigger is and what to do when they identify one
  • Clear escalation procedures — who do they call? Who completes the notice? Who approves pricing?
  • Regular review — update your template when legislation changes, when patterns in disputes reveal gaps, or when new technology offers better ways to capture evidence
  • Version control — date all template versions and ensure only current versions are used on active projects
